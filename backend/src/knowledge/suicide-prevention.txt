=== UNDERSTANDING SUICIDAL THOUGHTS ===
Suicidal thoughts, also known as suicidal ideation, are thoughts about ending one's life. These thoughts exist on a spectrum — from brief thoughts that life is not worth living to detailed plans. Having these thoughts does not mean a person will act on them, but they should always be taken seriously.

Suicidal thoughts are not a character flaw or sign of weakness. They are a signal that a person is experiencing more pain than they currently feel able to cope with. Help is available.

If you or someone you know is having thoughts of suicide, please contact a crisis helpline immediately.

=== WARNING SIGNS ===
Warning signs that someone may be at risk include:
- Talking about wanting to die or to kill themselves
- Looking for ways to kill themselves (searching online, collecting pills)
- Talking about being a burden to others
- Increased use of alcohol or drugs
- Withdrawing from friends, family, and activities
- Extreme mood swings
- Expressing hopelessness or having no reason to live
- Giving away prized possessions
- Saying goodbye as if it were the last time

=== RISK FACTORS ===
Risk factors are not causes, but they can increase vulnerability. They include:
- A prior suicide attempt (this is one of the strongest risk factors)
- Mental health conditions, particularly depression
- Family history of suicide
- Access to lethal means (firearms, medications)
- Recent loss — job, relationship, loved one
- Chronic pain or illness
- Trauma or abuse history
- Social isolation and loneliness

Having risk factors does not mean a person will attempt suicide. Many people with risk factors never do. This information is educational and NOT a diagnostic checklist.

=== PROTECTIVE FACTORS ===
Protective factors can reduce the likelihood of suicidal behaviour:
- Strong social connections and support
- Access to mental health care
- Sense of purpose and meaning
- Problem-solving and coping skills
- Reasons for living
- Religious or cultural beliefs against suicide
- Restricted access to lethal means

=== HOW TO HELP SOMEONE YOU ARE WORRIED ABOUT ===
If you are concerned about someone:

1. Ask directly: "Are you thinking about suicide?" Research shows asking does not plant the idea — it can provide relief.

2. Listen: Let them talk without judgment. You do not need to fix everything. Just being present matters.

3. Show you care: "I am glad you told me. I want to help you."

4. Stay with them: Do not leave them alone if they are in immediate danger.

5. Help them connect: Offer to call a helpline with them or help them make an appointment with a professional.

6. Remove access to means: If possible and safe, remove access to firearms, medications, or other means.

7. Follow up: Check in with them regularly. Recovery takes time.

=== WHAT TO SAY AND WHAT TO AVOID ===

Things to say:
- "I am here for you."
- "You are not alone."
- "I care about you."
- "This pain can get better with support."
- "Can I help you connect with someone?"

Things to avoid:
- "You have so much to live for." (invalidating)
- "Think about what this will do to your family." (guilt)
- "It will get better." (dismissive)
- "You are being selfish." (harmful)

=== GRIEF AFTER SUICIDE LOSS ===
Losing someone to suicide is a traumatic and complex grief. Survivors often experience guilt, anger, confusion, and profound sadness. Grief after suicide loss is different from other types of bereavement.

Support resources for those who have lost someone to suicide:
- Survivors of Bereavement by Suicide (UK): https://uksobs.org
- Alliance of Hope for Suicide Loss Survivors: https://allianceofhope.org
- iCall (India): 9152987821

Professional counselling, support groups, and peer support are all helpful. You are not alone.
