=== WHAT IS SELF-CARE? ===
Self-care refers to intentional actions you take to care for your mental, emotional, and physical health. It is not selfish — it is necessary. You cannot pour from an empty cup. Self-care helps build resilience, manage stress, and maintain mental wellness.

Effective self-care is personal. What works for one person may not work for another. Experiment and find what helps you feel restored.

=== PHYSICAL SELF-CARE ===
Physical health and mental health are deeply connected. Caring for your body supports your mind.

Exercise: Regular physical activity is one of the most evidence-backed interventions for depression and anxiety. Even 20–30 minutes of moderate exercise three times per week can significantly improve mood. Walking, yoga, swimming, and dancing all count.

Sleep: Prioritise 7–9 hours per night for adults. Poor sleep worsens every mental health condition. See sleep hygiene tips in the fundamentals document.

Nutrition: A balanced diet with plenty of fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and lean protein supports brain health. Limit alcohol, which is a central nervous system depressant.

Hydration: Dehydration affects concentration, mood, and energy. Aim for 2–3 litres of water daily.

=== EMOTIONAL SELF-CARE ===
Emotional self-care means paying attention to your emotional needs and processing your feelings rather than suppressing them.

Journaling: Writing about your thoughts and feelings can help process emotions, identify patterns, and gain clarity. There is no right way to journal.

Gratitude practice: Writing 3 things you are grateful for each day has been shown to improve mood and life satisfaction over time.

Self-compassion: Treat yourself with the same kindness you would offer a good friend. Self-criticism worsens mental health; self-compassion builds it.

Creative expression: Art, music, writing, and other creative activities can be powerful emotional outlets.

=== SOCIAL SELF-CARE ===
Human connection is a fundamental need. Social isolation is a major risk factor for poor mental health.

Nurture relationships: Invest time in relationships that feel supportive, honest, and mutual.

Seek community: Shared interest groups, volunteer work, or spiritual communities can provide belonging.

Set healthy boundaries: It is acceptable to limit contact with relationships that are consistently draining or harmful.

Ask for help: Seeking support is a sign of strength, not weakness.

=== MINDFULNESS PRACTICES ===
Box breathing (4-4-4-4):
1. Inhale slowly for 4 counts.
2. Hold for 4 counts.
3. Exhale slowly for 4 counts.
4. Hold for 4 counts.
5. Repeat 4 times.

Body scan:
Starting from the top of your head, slowly move attention through each part of your body, noticing sensations without judgment. Takes 5–20 minutes.

5-4-3-2-1 grounding (for anxiety/panic):
Notice 5 things you can see, 4 you can touch, 3 you can hear, 2 you can smell, 1 you can taste. Brings attention to the present moment.

Progressive muscle relaxation:
Systematically tense and release muscle groups from feet to face. Reduces physical tension associated with anxiety.

=== BUILDING RESILIENCE ===
Resilience is the ability to bounce back from adversity. It is not fixed — it can be developed.

Practices that build resilience:
- Maintaining perspective: difficult periods are temporary
- Building a support network
- Developing problem-solving skills
- Finding meaning and purpose
- Practicing acceptance of things outside your control
- Taking care of physical health

=== DIGITAL WELLNESS ===
Excessive social media use and screen time are associated with poorer mental health, particularly in young people.

Digital wellness tips:
- Set specific times to check social media (not first thing in the morning)
- Turn off non-essential notifications
- Curate your feed — unfollow accounts that make you feel worse
- Take regular screen breaks (20-20-20 rule: every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds)
- Create screen-free zones (bedroom, meal times)

=== PROFESSIONAL SUPPORT ===
Self-care strategies are valuable but are not substitutes for professional mental health care. If you are struggling:

- Speak with your doctor about mental health concerns
- Contact a mental health professional (psychologist, counsellor, psychiatrist)
